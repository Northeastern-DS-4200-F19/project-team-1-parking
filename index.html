Project Team #1: Parking, DS 4200 F19

Title: A day's worth of parking in Chester Square

Brian Fogarty, Ysabelle Kempe, Lal Birali, Victoria Yang

Service-Learning Course Project as part of DS 4200: Information Presentation and Visualization, taught by Prof. Cody Dunne, Data Visualization @ Khoury, Northeastern University.

Motivation

This project was developed in conjunction with the Chester Square Neighborhood Association. The goal of the Neighborhood Association is to show that there is a problem with parking availability and distribution in the area. Residents are often unable to find parking close to their homes, and there are complaints of inefficient parking practices.

The Neighborhood Association wants to bring the problem of parking to the attention of local government. The Association already knows there is a problem and is able to explain the issue and potential causes in words (e.g., not enough parking, residents donâ€™t know where to park, etc.), but they want to be able to show numbers that prove their point.

We hope this project serves as a valuable tool for the Neighborhood Association to communicate the need for change to authorities. Cars are a key player in daily urban life, and parking needs to be taken seriously in order to create a better standard of living for those on both sides of Chester Square.

When designing our project, we asked ourselves how occupied Chester Square parking is throughout the day. We wanted our final piece to show change over time, as well as give users an idea of how difficult it is to find parking at any given time. Through our own research and data collection, we have concluded that a reasonable response would be to more clearly mark parking spots, as well as conduct another survey.

Visualization explanation

Our visualization includes an interactive map showing occupancy rate, as well a bar chart that shows the empty and occupied parking spots on each street. The user is able to use a slider to change the time of day, which is reflected in the visualizations.

For our map, we used color to represent occupancy rate. We used a color scale of red, white, and green because we believed it was intuitive for red to mean that there are less spots available and green to mean there are more spots available. We designed the map to only show streets because we didn't want to clutter the visualizations with buildings or other landmarks.

We included the bar chart as well because it is very important for a user not only to know the proportion of spots filled, but to know exactly how many spots are left. We used a simple stacked bar chart to show this data, which uses area to show the parking capacity of a certain street, as well as area and color to show the breakdown of empty versus occupied spots.

For interactivity, users can use the slider to update both visualizations, as well as hover over the bar chart for more detailed information on the number of empty and occupied spots.

Data Analysis

Our data was collected during a parking survey conducted in Chester Square from 6 a.m. to 8 p.m. on Oct. 29, 2019.

Filled spots, empty spots, and blocked spots were recorded by hand on the hour. We filled this data into a Google Sheet by hand. This original Google Sheet included each spot with a unique id number (integer), the street (string), and the parking status for each spot throughout each hour of the data collection. If a car was parked in a spot, we used the first three characters of the license plate (string). If the spot was blocked, we used "blocked". If there was construction blocking the spot, we used the value "construction". If there was simply no car parked in the spot, we did not include a value in that cell.

For the bar chart data, we used Python to clean and preprocess. We iterated through the data and aggregated the number of occupied spots and full capacity for each street. The final data has street name as a string, occupied spots as an integer, and capacity as an integer for each time of day.

For the map data, we also used Python with the NumPy and Pandas libraries to clean and preprocess. The output was a csv file with polygon coordinates (floats), occupancy counts (integers), occupancy rates (floats), and percentages (floats).

Design Process

Below, you can see our initial visualization draft. Originally, we had imagined the color scale as red, yellow, and green. Spatial position is one of the most valuable encodings in data visualization, and we wanted to use a map to show where exactly the problem areas are for parking. We simplified the map down to just streets (rather than including landmarks and buildings) because we wanted to avoid chart clutter. In this original sketch, we imagined that the bar chart on the bottom would show the number of available spots in the area at each hour. We did this because we wanted our vis to be very practical and usable in the goal of showing people how easy it is to find parking at a specific time. Color coding on this bar chart would have corresponded to the color scale for the map, with green meaning more spots are available and red meaning less spots are available. A slider in this sketch controls the time of day currently shown on the map. For each selected time of day, that bar on the bar chart would have been highlighted.

Our next visualization sketch, shown below, was done digitally using Tableau and Adobe Illustrator. We made a few very substantial changes in this iteration. One of these changes was to alter our color scale from red, yellow, and green to red, white, and green. We belive that this color scale is more intuitive because some people may not associate yellow with a midpoint between green and red. White is more neutral. We toyed with the idea of showing individual cars on our map rather than occupancy rates, but we decided against it because we wanted to keep the vis simple enough for users to gain a broader main takeaway. We altered our bar chart to a stacked bar chart that shows the number of occupied and empty spots for each street, rather than for each time of day. As the slider updates the time shown on the map, the stacked bar chart also updates. This makes our visualization more information-dense without too much added complexity. We nixed color on the bar chart because we did not feel it was intuitive to have the same color scale for both visualization portions since they are showing different aspects of our data. We added a filter option for users to choose which types of regulation spots they wanted to see. This would help make the visualization customizable, more granular, and show varying levels of complexity.

Our final visualization contains our planned map and bar chart, with the slider to update time of day. One aspect of our plan that we were not able to implement due to time constraints was filtering based on regulation. However, we still believe that this visualization gives a good overview of a day of parking in Chester Square.

Unfortunately, our group was unable to take part in the in-class usability testing. In fact, we hit quite a few roadblocks to creating our final product. Due to time constraints, we were forced to kill our darlings. In other words, we had to cut some features of the visualization that, given more time, we would have included.

Conclusion

Parking is an extraordinarily important part of urban life. It allows people to own cars and have greater freedom of transportation. But, especially in a city like Boston, real estate is incredibly valuable and cars take up a lot of space.

This parking survey and the following vis was designed to identify parking problem areas in Chester Square, analyze how efficiently people park, and prompt ideas for improving parking in the neighborhood. When we began this project, none of us had any experience with parking analysis or parking surveys, but throughout the course of this project we began to understand how bad parking or bad parking spot design can have resonating effects on the layout of a neighborhood. Parking availability can vastly effect quality of life for residents because it could mean the difference between going straight home after a long day at work or having to trudge for 10 minutes in the cold darkness to get home.

Through our experiences walking around Chester Square, as well as our data analysis, we discovered that there are parking issues in the neighborhood. There were many people who parked very far away from eachother, but did not leave enough space for another car to park. A suggestion we have is for local government to add painted parking spots onto the street so that people are directed to park more efficiently.

For further analysis, we would also recommend a follow-up parking survey be performed on a day with more ideal conditions. We were forced to work on a rainy day when there was construction, which skewed our results.
